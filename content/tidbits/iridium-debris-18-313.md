On June 24, 2024, **Iridium** filed a letter in FCC docket 18-313 — *Mitigation of Orbital
Debris in the New Space Age* — urging the Commission to do something it has so far avoided
pinning down: set a **firm threshold for accidental explosions** of satellites, in accordance
with NASA Standards. Iridium argues the limit can't just exist on paper — it "must be applied
evenly" and clearly defined "so that it may be calculated and enforced" — and urges the FCC to
seek comment on exactly how.

**Why it matters.** Limiting accidental explosions is part of what docket 18-313 — *Mitigation
of Orbital Debris in the New Space Age* — is about, and Iridium wants the rule made concrete: a
hard, NASA-standards-based number (NASA-STD-8719.14) written in rather than left to discretion.
It's a crowded fight: docket 18-313 holds **339 filings**, including 159 comments and 125 ex
parte notices.

> "The Earth is the cradle of humanity, but mankind cannot stay in the cradle forever."
> — Konstantin Tsiolkovsky

We can leave the cradle — but not if we ring it with shrapnel first. Iridium's letter is an
argument that the door out of the cradle stays open only if we agree, in writing, not to blow
things up on the threshold.
